Welcome

Casey Trees is a Washington, D.C. - based nonprofit committed to restoring, enhancing and protecting the tree canopy of our nation’s capital. We pursue our mission through community action, education and research.

Casey Trees’ Tree Report Card measures the quantity and condition of D.C.’s trees and the collective efforts of all groups and individuals working to achieve the District’s 40 percent tree canopy goal. It is based on data collected from both public and private sources, including the District and Federal Governments, universities, developers, nonprofits and others.

Summary

Executive Summary

Spring is a time for new growth, but before we look forward toward reaching our 40 percent tree canopy goal, we must look back. The Casey Trees annual Tree Report Card reviews and highlights the successes of our previous year and calls attention to where we can do more. I am happy to report that our tree canopy has continued to improve; we exceeded our tree planting goals and a majority of the trees in the District are still in good or excellent health. However, many of our trees are still at constant risk for removal and we continue to see impervious surfaces dominate our landscape.

The theme for this year’s Tree Report Card is Empowering Future Generations Through Trees. To grow a greater, greener city, we must work together, not just today or tomorrow, but day after day and year after year. When we teach younger generations about the value of trees and green space now, we will ensure that our city’s lush tree filled legacy continues.

I want to thank everyone who has been involved in restoring, enhancing and protecting the District’s tree canopy over the past year. It is through your involvement, donations and hard work that we are, and will continue to be, the “City of Trees”.

Thanks as always for your support!

Spotlight: Empowering Future Generations Through Trees

Background

It is essential that younger generations feel vested to protect their trees and green space which happens when they feel a connection to it. Connecting with the environment and finding value in it can be done in many different ways, but it all starts with going outside. Providing children, a place where they can interact with their local environment is important, but they cannot do this if there is not a healthy and comfortable space to do it. Trees can create these spaces.

Our trees are one of our city’s most valuable resources. It can take generations for a tree to grow to maturity, but only a moment for it to be cut down. When a large tree is removed, it can significantly impact an entire community, not just because of the environmental benefits lost, but because of the intrinsic value trees bring. If we are going to continue to protect our trees from illegal removal, we must make sure that kids are getting a chance to interact with them and experience, not only the aesthetic value they provide, but also the social and health benefits that come with having a strong and vibrant urban environment.

Empower to be Healthy

Playgrounds are a place for kids to experience their local environment through outdoor activities. Unfortunately, on a hot summer day, the urban heat island effect makes the plastic and concrete that these spaces are made of extremely hot and ultimately unusable. Maintaining trees and green spaces around play structures creates a cool and welcoming environment that kids are comfortable playing in.

Using outdoor spaces increases a child’s overall physical activity, decreasing the potential for childhood obesity and other chronic illnesses, helps them develop stronger bones and immune systems and improves their sensory development and spatial awareness, all while allowing them to connect with their surrounding environment. When we empower kids to go outside and play, we are promoting their overall health and their appreciation of the natural world that they are playing in.

Empower to Grow

When kids have a space they can play in, they also have a space where they can grow and develop. Having comfortable places to play gives kids chances to interact, helping them develop social skills such as communication, listening and conflict-resolution. Doing this also helps kids create a community and develop their own sense of place within their local environment. When we empower kids to create their community in a shared space, we are also empowering them to work together to protect that space in the future.

Using outdoor spaces increases a child’s overall physical activity, decreasing the potential for childhood obesity and other chronic illnesses, helps them develop stronger bones and immune systems and improves their sensory development and spatial awareness, all while allowing them to connect with their surrounding environment. When we empower kids to go outside and play, we are promoting their overall health and their appreciation of the natural world that they are playing in.

Empower to Lead

When children are taken outdoors, they are given a chance to explore the natural world and learn about the value of their local environment. Environmental education is an important part of a child’s education and kids who are given the opportunity to go outside and have hands on learning experiences, whether it is with a class or with a friend, in a city or a small town, are more likely to be self-motivated, creative and critical thinkers and confident in their abilities. Together, these skills create strong leaders. When we empower kids to go outside and learn, we are also teaching them the skills necessary to become well rounded future leaders that will understand the value of their environment.

Metrics Explained

   

The Tree Report Card rates Washington, D.C.’s urban forest based on four metrics: Tree Coverage, Tree Health, Tree Planting and Tree Protection. Each one of these metrics is evaluated and given a letter grade. The overall grade for the District’s tree canopy is the average of these scores. This year, the average grade for the District’s tree canopy is an A-.

Tree Coverage

This metric measures the tree crown or canopy when looked at from an aerial view. Every five years, Casey Trees uses satellite imaging to measure the growth of the District’s tree canopy. Tracking tree coverage ensures that we remain on track to reach our 40 percent tree canopy goal by 2032. When last measured in 2016, the District had 38 percent tree canopy coverage. For this reason, Tree Coverage receives an A grade.

Tree Health

This metric evaluates the condition of trees across the District. Tree size and species are used to determine its overall health. This information reflects how resilient the canopy can be when faced with pests and disease. Every five years, Casey Trees evaluates the District’s tree health. This is done by surveying 201 sites across the District and assessing the health of the trees on these plots of land.

The 2015 survey results showed little change in tree health from the 2010 results, with most trees being in good to excellent health. Tree Health receives a B- grade.

Tree Planting

This metric states the number of trees planted each year on both public and private land. Tree planting is important because it allows us to both grow and maintain our tree canopy.

In 2019, there were 13,748 trees planted throughout the District. This is 3,248 more than the District’s annual tree planting goal of 10,500. For the city to achieve its 40 percent tree canopy goal by 2032, at least 10,648 trees must be planted each year. We have surpassed this number for the last 5 years. For this reason, Tree Planting for 2019 receives an A+ grade.

Tree Protection

Unlike the tree coverage, health and planting metrics, the Tree Protection metric evaluates the success of tree law in protecting trees. The grade for tree protection is measured by averaging the score of submetrics.

We use three submetrics to assess the implementation and the enforcement of the 2002 Urban Forest Preservation Act and the 2016 Tree Canopy Protection Amendment Act, which created Special and Heritage trees.

These designations provide protection for mature trees at risk of being removed by requiring people or nongovernmental entities to obtain permits if they wish to remove Special trees, making it illegal to remove Heritage trees and creating fines for noncompliance. Mature trees are the ones that provide the most benefits to the District and without having successful tree protection policies; these trees are at constant risk for removal.

Fee Adjustment: Are the fees and fines levied keeping pace with inflation?
Using the Consumer Price Index, we can see that inflation from 2016 to 2019 increased the Special Tree Removal Permit fee from $55 per inch circumference to $59.66. Per inch, this is not a significant difference. However, increasing this fee to meet inflation would have resulted in $140,779.58 more being collected. This submetric meets the threshold of an A-.

Fee Management: Are fees and fines being used to plant replacement trees?
The Urban Forest Preservation Act states that the money collected from the Special Tree Removal Permit fees and illegal tree removal fines must be used to plant trees on public and private land. This money continues to be used for this purpose. Therefore, we are giving this submetric an A+.

Tree Survival: Are the replacement trees surviving?
Tree survival data shows us that two out of every ten trees planted in an urban setting grow to Special tree size. The amount of money that is received for every Special tree removed is enough to plant 10 new trees. At least 2 of those ten trees planted will survive and grow to become mature special tree sized.

The average of these three submetrics results in an A- for Tree Protection.

Recommendations

To empower future generations,

1

Protecting open spaces from being developed by working with the D.C. Council, District agencies and stakeholders to identify city-owned greenspace that should be conserved forever.

2

Expanding the Urban Forest Preservation Act to protect all trees of Special and Heritage tree size, no matter if they are on public or private land.

3

Updating the Environmental Literacy Plan and ensure all children throughout the District are receiving the necessary environmental education both in-classroom and outdoors in order to ensure the long-term stewardship of the canopy.

4

Adopting a city-wide impervious surface maximum percentage to protect open spaces from being developed and ensure a lush city for years to come.

Appendix: Calculations Defined

 

Tree Coverage is a measure of a tree’s crown when viewed from above and is the way we track the progress that the District makes towards its 40 percent tree canopy by 2032 goal. The tree crown or canopy produces most of the trees overall benefits.

Tree Health encompasses trees condition, species, size and type. This metric helps us understand how resilient the District’s trees are in terms of potential threats due to pests and diseases. It can also predict the longevity and future composition of D.C.’s trees.

Tree Planting is a count of annual tree planting numbers compared to the total number of trees that must be planted per year to achieve the city’s 40 percent canopy goal by 2032.

Tree Protection assesses the impact of the Urban Forestry Preservation Act of 2002, and the Tree Canopy Protection Amendment Act of 2016. These laws are intended to slow the removal of healthy trees 44 - 100 inches in circumference and ensure their replacement when they are cut down. Additionally, they prohibit the removal of trees over 100 inches in circumference unless granted special approval by the Mayor.
